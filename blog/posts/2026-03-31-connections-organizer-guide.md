# Connections Organizer: A Free Tool to Sort and Solve NYT Connections

The NYT Connections puzzle gives you 16 words and asks you to sort them into four groups of four. Sounds simple, right? It's not. The words are deliberately chosen to have overlapping possible connections, and you only get four mistakes before it's game over.

I built the [Connections Organizer](https://tylervibes.com/connections_organizer/connectionsorganizer.html) to give your brain some breathing room.

## What Is It?

The Connections Organizer is a drag-and-drop workspace where you can visually sort the 16 puzzle words into groups. Instead of trying to hold four possible categories in your head while staring at a flat grid, you can physically move words around, test groupings, and see patterns emerge.

## How to Use It

### Step 1: Enter the Words

Type all 16 words from today's Connections puzzle into the input grid. Each word gets its own box, and hitting Enter automatically advances to the next one.

### Step 2: Start Organizing

Once you click "Start Organizing," you'll see two panels:

- **Available Words** on the right — all 16 words as draggable cards
- **Category Groups** on the left — four labeled groups where you can drop words

### Step 3: Drag and Drop

Drag words from the available pool into whichever group you think they belong to. You can move words between groups as you change your mind.

Each group gets a **color selector** (yellow, green, blue, purple — matching the NYT Connections colors) and a **name field** where you can label what you think the connection is. This is surprisingly helpful. Naming the connection forces you to commit to a theory, which often reveals whether your grouping actually makes sense.

### Step 4: Refine and Solve

The magic is in the rearranging. Maybe you put "BASS" in the music group, but then you realize it could also be a fish. Move it over, see if the fish group holds up. The physical act of shuffling words around activates a different kind of thinking than just staring at them.

There's also a **shuffle** button if you want to randomize the remaining words — sometimes a fresh arrangement is all you need to spot a pattern.

## Tips for Solving Connections

Here's what I've learned from using this tool daily:

- **Start with the group you're most confident about.** Lock in the easy one first to reduce the noise.
- **Watch for red herrings.** The puzzle designers intentionally place words that *could* fit multiple groups. If a word seems too obvious, question it.
- **Name your groups.** If you can't name the connection, you probably don't have the right grouping.
- **Use the purple group as your anchor.** Purple is always the trickiest — if you can identify those four words, the rest falls into place.
